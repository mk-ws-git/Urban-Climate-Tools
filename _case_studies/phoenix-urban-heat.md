## Challenge

Phoenix is the hottest large city in the United States, with summer temperatures regularly exceeding 110F (43C). The urban heat island effect amplifies already extreme temperatures, creating serious public health risks and enormous energy demands for cooling.

### The Problem

- **Extreme heat**: Average of 110 days per year above 100F
- **Heat-related deaths**: Hundreds of fatalities annually
- **Energy burden**: Cooling costs strain low-income households
- **Nighttime heat**: Urban surfaces retain heat, preventing overnight cooling
- **Limited tree canopy**: Only 9% coverage compared to 20%+ in other cities

## Solution Approach

Phoenix launched a comprehensive heat mitigation strategy combining cool surfaces, urban forestry, public health interventions, and policy changes.

### 1. Cool Surface Program

Using the Cool Roofs Calculator, Phoenix identified priority buildings and incentivized cool roof installations across residential and commercial properties.

### 2. Urban Forestry Initiative

Committed to planting 4 million trees over 10 years, with priority given to heat-vulnerable neighborhoods and transit corridors.

### 3. Public Health Response

Established cooling centers, developed extreme heat response protocols, and launched public education campaigns.

### 4. Energy Efficiency

Partnered with utilities to reduce peak demand through efficiency programs and demand response systems.

## Key Outcomes

### Environmental Impact
- **2,000+ buildings** retrofitted with cool roofs
- **Surface temperatures reduced** by up to 50F on cool roofs
- **4M trees** in planting pipeline
- **8% reduction** in peak electricity demand

### Health Impact
- **Expanded cooling network**: 200+ cooling centers accessible
- **Heat response system**: Early warning and intervention protocols
- **Vulnerable population outreach**: Direct engagement with at-risk communities

### Economic Impact
- **Energy savings**: $50M+ in reduced cooling costs
- **Green jobs**: 500+ positions in heat mitigation programs
- **Property values**: Improved in heavily shaded neighborhoods

## Lessons Learned

1. **Prioritize by vulnerability**: Focus investments where heat risks are highest
2. **Combine strategies**: No single solution addresses extreme heat
3. **Plan for the long term**: Trees take decades to mature
4. **Engage utilities**: Energy partnerships amplify impact

## Replication Potential

Phoenix's approach is directly applicable to other hot-climate cities. Cool roofs and urban forestry can be adapted to local conditions, while the public health framework provides a model for emergency response.
