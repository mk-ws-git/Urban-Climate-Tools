## Challenge

Minneapolis faces significant climate challenges including urban heat islands, stormwater flooding, and environmental inequities. Low-income neighborhoods and communities of color have historically been underserved by green space investments, leading to disparities in heat exposure and flood risk.

### The Problem

- **Urban heat disparities**: Some neighborhoods are 10F hotter than others
- **Stormwater flooding**: Aging infrastructure unable to handle increased precipitation
- **Environmental injustice**: Green space concentrated in wealthier areas
- **Health impacts**: Higher rates of heat-related illness in underserved communities

## Solution Approach

Minneapolis developed an equity-focused green infrastructure program that prioritizes investments in historically underserved neighborhoods while creating local economic opportunities.

### 1. Community-Led Planning

Working directly with neighborhood organizations to identify priorities and design solutions that meet community needs while addressing climate challenges.

### 2. Green Infrastructure Implementation

- **Rain gardens**: Over 500 installed in residential and commercial areas
- **Permeable pavement**: Replaced impervious surfaces in priority corridors
- **Urban tree canopy**: Focused planting in low-canopy neighborhoods
- **Green schoolyards**: Transformed 30 school grounds into green spaces

### 3. Workforce Development

Created green jobs training programs in partnership with community colleges and workforce development organizations, prioritizing residents from target neighborhoods.

## Key Outcomes

### Environmental Impact
- **150+ green infrastructure projects** completed
- **2-3C temperature reduction** in pilot areas
- **40% stormwater runoff reduction** in project areas
- **5,000 trees planted** in priority neighborhoods

### Economic Impact
- **$45M in green jobs** created
- **200+ local residents** trained in green infrastructure maintenance
- **Local procurement**: 60% of contracts to local businesses

### Social Impact
- **Community ownership**: Residents involved in design and stewardship
- **Health co-benefits**: Improved air quality and reduced heat exposure
- **Educational programming**: Climate curriculum in participating schools

## Lessons Learned

1. **Equity must be intentional**: Without explicit focus, investments tend to flow to wealthier areas
2. **Community partnerships are essential**: Local organizations bring trust and local knowledge
3. **Economic opportunity multiplies impact**: Green jobs create lasting community investment
4. **Data transparency builds accountability**: Public tracking of investments by neighborhood

## Replication Potential

Minneapolis's model can be adapted by other cities seeking to address climate and equity simultaneously. Key transferable elements include community engagement frameworks, equity metrics, and workforce development partnerships.
